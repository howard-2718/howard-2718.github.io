Proof

By differentiating \(y = ax^2 + bx + c\) twice, notice that

\begin{align*} \amp y = ax^2 + bx + c\\ \Longleftrightarrow \; \amp y' = 2ax + b\\ \Longleftrightarrow \; \amp y'' = 2a. \end{align*}

To find the vertex, we consider values of \(x\) for which \(y' = 0\text{.}\) With minimal rearrangement, we see that

\begin{equation*} x = -\frac{b}{2a}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(a\) is nonzero, it follows that \(y'' = 2a\) is nonzero for all \(x\text{,}\) which confirms the existence of an extrema at the \(x\)-coordinate in question. \(\blacksquare\)
